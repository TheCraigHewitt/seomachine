# Freaking Nomads — Brand Voice & Messaging

This document defines the Freaking Nomads brand voice, tone, and messaging framework. Reference this for every piece of content to ensure consistency. When in doubt: would a nomad friend who's actually been there say this? If not, rewrite it.

---

## Who We Are

**Freaking Nomads** is a digital nomad publication, community, and platform built by nomads for nomads. We challenge the status quo of digital nomadism — debunking stereotypes, dispelling myths, and providing unfiltered insights by collaborating with real nomads to bring forth authentic stories.

**Co-founders**: Irene Lidia Wang and Luca Mussari

## Brand Voice Pillars

### 1. Brutally Honest
- **What it means**: We tell it like it is. We acknowledge problems, limitations, and trade-offs upfront — not buried at the end. We don't oversell destinations, products, or the nomad lifestyle.
- **How it sounds**: Direct, unhedged, specific. We say "the internet in Mazunte can be hit or miss" not "connectivity may vary." We say "Finland's processing time is around 4 months" not "can take some time."
- **Example**: "I'd choose Mazunte again in a heartbeat — though perhaps not for the long haul."
- **Avoid**: Vague praise, marketing language, false balance, burying the lead, softening bad news with "however" or "that said"

### 2. Personal & Grounded
- **What it means**: Everything is filtered through lived experience. We write as nomads who have been there, tested the product, filed the claim, sat in that café. We name specific places, real prices, actual speeds.
- **How it sounds**: First-person throughout, personal credentials upfront, specific data over generalizations.
- **Example**: "I started using it while traveling because I wanted data that didn't expire." / "Shanghai: 79.31 Mbps download / 14.96 Mbps upload. Hangzhou: 56.11 Mbps / 12.23 Mbps."
- **Avoid**: "Many nomads find..." / "It is generally recommended..." / Vague claims like "fast connectivity" or "affordable living"

### 3. Friendly but Straight-Talking
- **What it means**: We sound like a friend who has been there and done that — not a travel agency or a marketing team. Warm, occasionally cheeky, never try-hard. We use "you" constantly.
- **How it sounds**: Conversational, casual sentence fragments are fine, contractions always, rhetorical questions where natural.
- **Example**: "Ever had an immigration officer ask for proof of onward travel and your stomach just drops?" / "Dummy tickets are not fake! They are genuine temporary airline reservations."
- **Avoid**: Corporate tone, formal transitions ("Furthermore," "Moreover,"), stilted phrasing, trying too hard to be funny

### 4. Balanced (Attractions + Friction)
- **What it means**: We show both sides with equal weight. For destination guides, pros and cons get equal real estate. For product reviews, we state negatives plainly without softening.
- **How it sounds**: Skepticism-first on destinations, honest assessment in reviews. We state the verdict clearly: "For most people, we think the Nexstand K2 comes out just slightly ahead."
- **Example**: "Sal is definitely a good nomad destination — we recommend 10 days (2 weeks maximum)." / "The stylus isn't the best when compared to competing drawing tools."
- **Avoid**: Overselling, unearned enthusiasm, pros/cons sections where cons are clearly afterthoughts

### 5. Specific, Not General
- **What it means**: Numbers, names, and places replace vague claims. We don't say "affordable" — we say "$1,121/month in Chiang Mai." We don't say "good internet" — we say "20–50 Mbps."
- **How it sounds**: Data-dense in a readable way. Specific neighborhoods, café names, exact prices, real speeds.
- **Example**: "A 48-hour ticket starts at $14 USD, while extended validity up to two weeks costs around $30 USD." / "Chiang Mai: $370 accommodation + $240 transport + $150 dining + $80 groceries = $1,121 total."
- **Avoid**: "Great value," "reliable speeds," "vibrant community" (without specifics)

## Tone by Content Type

**Destination Guides**
- Skepticism-first opener, then personal experience framing
- Equal weight to pros and cons
- Monthly cost breakdown (specific numbers)
- Name actual cafés, coworking spaces, neighborhoods
- End with honest verdict on who this destination is for and ideal stay length

**Product & Gear Reviews**
- Quick verdict or hook upfront
- Real-world testing credentials stated early
- Spec comparison tables
- Honest cons, stated plainly
- "Who this is for" framing at the end

**Visa Guides**
- Answer "Does [country] have a digital nomad visa?" directly in the first paragraph — no teasing
- Requirements table, step-by-step process, exact costs
- Tax implications
- Honest workaround if no dedicated visa exists

**Best-Of Listicles**
- Consistent structure for every item (description, price, pros/cons, who it's for)
- No padding, no superlatives without supporting data
- Personal picks flagged clearly

**Insurance & Finance Reviews**
- Lead with personal claim or usage experience
- Coverage numbers and hard limits
- Honest competitor comparison
- Clear "who should use this" verdict

**Trend & Opinion Pieces (Luca's voice)**
- Slightly more analytical, less personal anecdote
- Big-picture nomad landscape perspective
- Direct assertions, not hedged takes
- Example: "Digital nomadism in 2026 is going to feel very different from how it felt a few years ago."

**Lifestyle & Wellness (Irene's voice)**
- Opens with personal experience, warm but direct
- Practical advice grounded in her own nomad routine
- Example: "Yoga's become a non-negotiable part of my routine as a digital nomad."

## Writing Style Rules

### Sentence Structure
- **Vary length**: Short punchy sentences. Then a longer one that explains why or adds nuance. Then short again.
- **Active voice always**: "We tested the eSIM across three months in China" not "The eSIM was tested"
- **Contractions**: Always. "you're," "we've," "it's," "they're"
- **Fragments are fine**: "No hidden fees. No surprises. Just a card that works."
- **Rhetorical questions where natural**: "Sound too good to be true? It's not."

### Paragraph Structure
- 2–4 sentences max per paragraph
- One idea per paragraph
- No walls of text — especially important for mobile readers

### Word Choice
- Address the reader as "you" — always
- Use simple words: "use" not "utilize," "start" not "commence," "show" not "demonstrate"
- Specific over general: "$14 USD" not "affordable," "79 Mbps" not "fast"
- Active verbs: test, book, save, avoid, choose, skip

## Banned Words & Phrases

These make content sound AI-generated. Never use them:

| Banned | Why |
|--------|-----|
| delve | Instantly sounds AI |
| realm | Corporate/AI filler |
| unleash | Marketing cliché |
| landscape (metaphorical) | "The visa landscape" — no |
| navigate (metaphorical) | "Navigate the challenges" — no |
| embark | "Embark on your journey" — never |
| firsthand | Just say "I tested it" |
| moreover | Use "also" or start a new sentence |
| furthermore | Same |
| in conclusion | Just conclude |
| it's worth noting | Just say the thing |
| it's important to note | Same |
| comprehensive guide | Let the content prove it |
| game-changer | Overused, empty |
| deep dive | Say "we'll cover" or "this guide explains" |
| seamless | Every product claims this |
| robust | Filler adjective |
| leverage | Use "use" |
| cutting-edge | Date-specific claims only |
| digital nomad lifestyle (as opener) | Too generic — be specific |

---

## What FN Never Does

- No "In conclusion..." or "To summarize..."
- No generic CTAs mid-article ("Click here to learn more")
- No passive voice
- No vague claims without data to back them up
- No overselling destinations — always include friction points
- No recommending a product without testing or real-world context
- No hedging when a direct opinion exists ("it could be said that..." → just say it)
- No affiliate disclosure buried at the bottom — if it exists, it's upfront

## Messaging Framework

### Core Messages

**1. Authenticity Over Aspiration**
- We tell the truth about nomad life — the slow internet, the unexpected costs, the lonely days — not just the Instagram highlight reel.
- Use when: introductions, about references, destination guides

**2. Built by Nomads, For Nomads**
- Our content comes from people who live this life. Not travel writers, not remote SEO teams. People who file visa applications, test eSIMs in China, pay $1,121/month in Chiang Mai.
- Use when: establishing credibility, author bylines, review contexts

**3. The Tools You Actually Need**
- We review and recommend gear, services, and destinations based on what actually works for remote work — not tourism.
- Use when: gear reviews, destination guides, remote work content

**4. Community That Gets It**
- 1,433+ members who are living this life. The newsletter, the forum, the events — for people who already chose this path, or are seriously considering it.
- Use when: CTAs, community references, newsletter mentions

### Value Propositions

**For aspiring nomads**: "Stop planning, start doing. We'll show you exactly how — with real costs, real visas, and real experiences."

**For current nomads**: "The destination guide that tells you the Wi-Fi speed, the monthly cost, and whether the internet cuts out in storms — not just the best sunset spots."

**For remote workers**: "Reviews written by people who actually work from these places, not journalists on press trips."

## Audience

### Primary: Aspiring and Active Digital Nomads
- Remote workers (employees and freelancers) considering or already living nomadically
- Ages roughly 25–45
- Have some income stability; are optimizing for freedom, not just saving money
- Skeptical of hype; want real information
- Care deeply about: internet reliability, cost of living, visa legality, finding community

### Secondary Audiences
- **Senior nomads**: Older adults exploring nomadic life — underserved, growing segment
- **Nomads from non-Western countries**: Russians, Brazilians, Filipinos — need visa info tailored to their passports
- **Black digital nomads**: Seeking inclusive destinations and community
- **Nomad-curious professionals**: Software engineers, UX designers, copywriters considering the jump

### What They Care About
1. Honest, specific information (not "it depends")
2. Visa clarity — what's actually legal, not what people get away with
3. Real cost-of-living breakdowns
4. Internet reliability (speeds, providers, backup options)
5. Community and not feeling isolated
6. Gear and tools that actually work on the road

### Pain Points
1. Generic travel content that ignores remote work realities
2. Outdated visa information
3. Accommodation recommendations without work-from-home context
4. Insurance reviews that don't discuss claims experience
5. Not knowing whether they can actually sustain the lifestyle financially

## Quality Checklist

Before publishing any content, verify:

- [ ] **Opens with a hook** — gut-punch, personal story, provocative question. Not "In today's world..."
- [ ] **Real data included** — prices, speeds, costs, processing times — not vague descriptors
- [ ] **Active voice throughout** — no passive constructions
- [ ] **Author attributed** — Irene, Luca, or named contributor with credentials
- [ ] **Cons stated plainly** — not softened or buried
- [ ] **No banned words** — check the list above
- [ ] **FAQ section included** — at least 4 PAA-style questions
- [ ] **Internal links** — minimum 3, using descriptive anchor text
- [ ] **External links** — minimum 2, authoritative sources (government pages, established publications)
- [ ] **Meta title + description** — written, under 155 characters for description
- [ ] **Schema recommendations** — Article + FAQPage at minimum
- [ ] **AI detection** — content should not score above 40% on AI detection tools; if it does, humanize before publishing
- [ ] **Humanization pass** — MANDATORY. Every article must go through this step before publishing

---

**The test**: Read it aloud. Does it sound like something a smart, well-traveled friend would say over a beer? Or does it sound like a marketing brochure? If the latter — rewrite it.
